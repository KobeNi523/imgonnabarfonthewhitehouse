Let Wisconsin Communities Raise Wages

Wisconsin is still stuck at a $7.25 minimum wage, the same floor it had in 2009. For someone working full-time, that lands at roughly $14,500 a year before taxes, which is nowhere close to what it takes to cover rent, food, transportation, and the rest of normal life.

The situation gets worse in higher-cost communities because a 2018 state law (AB 748) blocks cities and counties from setting a higher local minimum wage. That means places like Madison and Milwaukee cannot adjust wages to fit local realities.

What’s the Problem?

Representative Alex Joers called $7.25 “unrealistic and unaffordable” in 2026, and that tracks with the numbers. In Dane County, the MIT Living Wage Calculator estimates that a single adult needs roughly $24 an hour just to cover basics.

Statewide wage policy has not kept up with the real cost of living, and local governments are barred from stepping in by the 2018 law (AB 748). That leaves workers stuck with a floor that does not reflect what it actually costs to live in Wisconsin today.

AB 481

Restore Local Wage Authority

This bill would have repealed the 2018 preemption law (AB 748) and given cities and counties the ability to raise wages based on local conditions.

Read the bill page

AB 1158

Raise the State Minimum Wage

This proposal would have moved large employers to $15 an hour immediately and then gradually increased the wage floor to $20 by 2030. It also included increases for tipped workers and a repeal of the local preemption law (AB 748).

Read the bill summary

AB 726

Raise Pay for Incarcerated Workers

This bill would have required a much higher wage floor for incarcerated laborers, who currently can make only a few cents an hour.

ACLU-WI bill summary

Why This Matters

  • $7.25 an hour works out to less than $14,000 a year for many full-time workers.
  • Dane County’s living wage estimate is about $24 an hour for a single adult.
  • Many people do work jobs that pay the minimum wage, with a large share still earning under $15 an hour.
  • Local governments know local costs and should have room to respond when the state does not.
  • Incarcerated laborers should not be trapped in wage rates measured in cents. Representative Alex Joers cites this as an "exploitative and egregious practice".
